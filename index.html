Research

My research sits at the intersection of plant ecology, conservation, and climate change biology. I seek to understand how global change shapes plant biodiversity and to translate this knowledge into evidence-based conservation and restoration strategies. Central to my work is elucidating the ecological and evolutionary processes driving plant rarity and distribution across environmental gradients under climate change.

Much of this work is grounded in botanical gardens, where living and preserved collections provide rich ecological and historical data. This has fostered a particular interest in the critical role of botanical gardens in global and decolonizing conservation efforts. To address questions across micro- and macro-ecological scales, I combine fieldwork with greenhouse and laboratory experiments, advanced statistical modelling, and the integration of field, herbarium, and remote-sensing data. Collaboration is central to my approach, and I work closely with national and international institutions, as well as local and Indigenous communities and stakeholders.

Main research contributions

Defining climatic thresholds of the upper elevation limit of grasslands (i.e. grassline)

Using European summit vegetation-data, Bürli et al. (2021) provided the first climatic definition of the grassline, improving predictions of climate change impacts on alpine ecosystems.

Investigating variation of sex ratio and sexual dimorphism along climatic gradients

Using population and climatic data, Bürli et al. (2022) showed that sex ratio and sexual dimorphism in dioecious plants vary with climate, revealing sex-specific responses to environmental change.

Unpacking the drivers of plant rarity

In a series of studies, Bürli et al. (2023, 2024, 2025) examined how herbivory, genetic diversity, and propagule pressure influence plant rarity and reintroduction outcomes. The results challenge assumptions about defence and inbreeding in rare species, emphasizing the role of outcrossing and ecological similarity in conservation efforts of rare and common plant species.

Native forest restoration in Aotearoa New Zealand

In collaboration with Māori partners, Bürli et al. (in review) investigated factors supporting tree seedling survival in the restoration of native forests. The project integrates ecological, socio-cultural, and Indigenous knowledge to address climate change, biodiversity restoration, and ethical questions of reparative ecology.

Teaching

Teaching philosophy

Ecology and conservation are conceptually rich disciplines that require both analytical and creative thinking. Effective teaching in these fields therefore involves translating complex ideas into accessible concepts grounded in real-world examples. I often begin courses with questions rooted in local cultural and ecological landscapes, guiding students toward broader principles while emphasizing critical thinking, evidence evaluation, and integration across spatial and temporal scales. I primarily draw on the scientific literature rather than textbooks to engage students with current research and equip them for biodiversity stewardship.

In this context, teaching and research are viewed as mutually reinforcing, with case studies from my work and that of peers used to link applied research with ecological theory. It is important to me that an inclusive, collaborative learning environment is fostered through active learning and diverse assessments, with mentorship supporting students as they develop independent research projects. At the University of Graz, I supervise Master and Bachelor theses and have previously co-supervised several Master and Bachelor theses.
